## The Real Cost of Self-Building

<Callout type="warn" title="What is your engineering team actually working on?">
Every game studio reinvents the same set of wheels: check-in systems, currency ledgers, lottery pity counters, announcement popups. None of it differentiates your product — yet it absorbs enormous engineering capacity and sprint bandwidth.
</Callout>

Building live-ops infrastructure in-house typically means:

- **Repeated rework** — Check-in, points, leaderboards get rebuilt from scratch for every new game or app
- **High maintenance burden** — Dedicated backend engineers required to keep systems running; holiday campaigns still need dev tickets
- **Ops teams blocked on engineering** — Every reward tweak, every new announcement, every campaign change requires a ticket, a sprint, a deployment
- **No cross-product reuse** — Multiple products in your portfolio, but none share any infrastructure
- **Accumulating technical debt** — Rushed live-ops systems become unmaintainable over time, slowing future iteration

## Who Benefits Most

<Steps>
<Step>

### Small Teams Building Casual Games

Three to five engineers, no dedicated live-ops backend budget. You need check-in, lottery, and task systems, but can't afford to put headcount on infrastructure that isn't core gameplay.

ApolloKit gives a small team full live-ops capability from a single SDK integration so engineering stays focused on what makes your game unique.

</Step>
<Step>

### Mid-Sized Companies with Multiple Products

You're running several games or apps simultaneously, each needing similar live-ops functionality. Self-building means N systems and N times the maintenance cost.

ApolloKit's multi-project architecture puts all your products on shared infrastructure, with each ops team managing its own configuration independently and engineering integrating only once per product.

</Step>
<Step>

### Teams That Want to Iterate on Live-Ops Quickly

"This season let's try a storage box mechanic." "Can we add a segmented leaderboard?" In a self-build world, these requests queue behind sprint planning for weeks.

ApolloKit's configurable modules let PMs and ops teams set this up directly in the dashboard and ship the same day, with no engineering involvement.

</Step>
<Step>

### Non-Gaming Apps with Reward Mechanics

Points-based check-ins, membership tiers, redemption code activations — these patterns appear in e-commerce, community, and edtech products too. ApolloKit's modules are not game-specific and work equally well in any consumer-facing app with engagement mechanics.

</Step>
</Steps>

## The Deeper Value

Saving engineering hours is the surface benefit. The deeper shift is **the speed of operational decision-making**:

- Operations teams ship campaigns without depending on engineering sprints
- Product managers configure, test, and iterate on live-ops mechanics directly
- Engineers are freed from repetitive infrastructure work to focus on genuinely differentiated logic
- AI agents further compress the configuration learning curve, making "idea to live" a same-day event
